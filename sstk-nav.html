    AN UPGRADED NAVIGATION

    Redesigned the navigation allowing customers to find content quicker and to learn about all of our products.

    ×

    My role

    Design co-lead

    Duration

    1 year

    Activities

    UX/UI design, user research, design handoff

    Platform

    Desktop-web, mobile-web

    Overview

    The current navigation was centered around only content listing out the different asset types we had to offer. My lead designer identified several issues and a business opportunity and invited me to work on it with her to redesign the navigation to address the following items:

    • Losing revenue due to constant changes resulting in inconsistencies
    • Expanding product offerings so need to reconfigure our categories
    • Broken experience depending on screen size and language

    Challenges

    • Losing money due to constant changes resulting in inconsistencies
    • Expanding product offerings so need to reconfigure our categories
    • Broken experience depending on screen size and language

    Design approach

    Before starting on any work, we outlined a strategy together and divided up the activities. We then shared it with our product team for buy-in and feedback.

    Research

    Together with my lead designer, we worked on the research activities together and split the work 50/50. I handled the Baymard insights and card sort exercise and she worked on the SWOT analysis and tree study. The focus was on gaining deep insights into user expectations of how they categorize our offerings and hierarchy.

    The audit identified several issues such as cluttered design, accessibility problems, limited insights, user disempowerment, inconsistent interface, and unclear hierarchy within the dashboard.

    Card sort

    Based on the click data from Google analytics and consulting our SEO team to ensure our changes weren't going to negatively affect SEO, we revamped the categories and ran a closed card sort.

    Left: The site map of our navigation breakdown
    Right: The card sort test in UserZoom

    Tree study

    To ensure an intuitive understanding of the site‘s structure, I tested the proposed hierarchy with potential users through so-called tree-testing by giving them relevant tasks, such as "Where would you click if you wanted to join a theatre group?" Based on the test results, the sitemap has been adjusted accordingly.

    The tree study in UserZoom

    The summarized results from the tree test

    • Majority of the participants were able to correctly match the descriptions to the proposed level 1 categories
    • Less than half of the participants were able to correctly match the descriptions to the proposed level 2 categories.
    • Participants don't understand what some labels mean e.g. "TurboSquid". Therefore, iterate on labels for better comprehension

    Baymard guidelines Audit

    We used Baymard to audit our site against their best practices which helped us determine which guidelines were critical that we needed to implement such as longer hover delays and provide more information contextually like labe descriptions.

    BROKEN EXPERIENCE, EXPLAINED

    Some categories would be hidden depending on which language and screen size the user was on. For example, in Portuguese, on a screen size of 1280px, several key categories would be missing without being able to find it elsewhere.

    Depending on the screen size, some navigation items would be missing

    How might we intuitively present our expanding product offering allowing for discoverablilty to our customers through a seamless and consstent experience and as a result, increase our session success rate ultimately leading to higer revenue?

    DESIGN WORKSHOP

    We co-facilitated a one day workshop with our newly assigned product team and main stakeholders including engineers and senior leadership where we described the problems with our current navigation and the opportunity we had in front of us in providing more discovery opportunities for our growing product offerings.

    After taking them through the workshop process with lightning demos, Crazy 8s, we dot voted the best ideas that jumpstarted our design explorations.

    DESIGN EXPLORATIONS

    My lead designer and I divided the ideas taking one version each and sketched mid level fidelity designs. After multiple design reviews with our product and design managers, we landed on two concepts to user test.

    Version A
    This version used a side menu to navigate between the different subcategories.

    Version B
    This version had a full-width menu showing all the subcategories in one view.

    Earlier concepts

    We went wide with exploring different variations of the menu designs and took elements of what we thought worked best based on usage data and best practices.

    Large thumbnail with descriptions and visible categories on the right

    Icons with descriptions and visible categories on the bottom

    User testing results

    We set up unmoderated usability sessions via UserZoom mixing which version the participants saw first in order to avoid bias.

    Version A
    While the side menu was more organized, participants couldn't scan the contents as easily.

    Version B - WINNER!
    This version had a full-width menu showing all the subcategories making it easier to scan with less click interactions.

    Learnings

    • 57% of participants found Version B to be easier to navigate
    • 69% of participants found the larger menus seeing more visuals and descriptions more useful
    • 63% of participants found the "My stuff" section very relevant

    Engineer handoff

    Our engineer team was involved starting from participating in the design workshop to reviewing wireframes and providing feedback on feasibility to handing off designs including design documentation. We consistently provided design feedback and worked through any challenges during periodic demos and Slack threads.

    I specified what each element is and what happens when a user interacts with it along with the spacing between the elements for each major screen.

    EXPERIMENTS

    The experiment spanned for 14 days. Our expectations were that the results would either be the same or increase slightly compared to control. Due to the short run, the data wasn't statistically significant but it was still deemed a winner despite the minimal impact. The data was directional enough to still implement the bigger menus as the new control.

    Capstone project

    The Opportunity

    Dealer Market Exchange was creating the car industry's first business mobility solutions platform. They had finished building its core features in its enterprise solution for automotive businesses. There focus turned to launching their online auction platform to allow users to conveniently bid on vehicles with a few clicks.

    I was tasked with creating their notification system initially for auctions but futureproof it for other features launching later.

    How might we help users receive timely notifications and immediately take action when participating in an online auction?

    RESEARCH

    Discovery

    The discovery phase allowed me to define project milestones, critique the exisiting app, review the competitor landscape, understand the user's needs, and research behaviors and pain-points.

    The research revealed very different results than anticipated. The main reason for using the app was the ticketing function and the occassional transit tracking.

    I had my own ideas going into the project of how the app should be improved but after doing some user research, I quickly learned that users don't use the app to its fullest potential. The primary use for the app is ticketing and purchasing tickets because users can buy them at any time.

    TARGET AUDIENCE

    The target audience was very clear: Most of Dart’s customers are commuters and we targeted users who are familiar with technology and use the original DART app as their primary way of validating their rides.

    USER INTERVIEWS

    I conducted a range of interviews with users who fit the target audience including co-workers and friends. The interview consisted of open and closed questions to learn about how they use the app and their pain-points. A combination of questionnaire and interviews brought insight into the needs of our users and gave me a better understanding of the usage and workflow.

    CREATING PERSONAS

    I translated the research I gathered into creating personas so there was a clear understanding of who my users were, their goals and motivations. The personas were constantly referenced throughout the project to guide design decisions, priorities and create empathy for the users.

    COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

    This was extremely insightful in understanding what was currently out on the market and what competitors implemented into their apps.

    I analyzed the pros and cons of each app and what made it work and why they prioritized certain features over the others. Some were very visual and some were bluntly utilitarian. All focused on real-time tracking methods but only a few were visual on a map. Nearly every app had a favorites option to access their most frequent stops and a Nearby feature showing what stops are nearest their current location.

    EXPLORATIONS

    My vision for the DART app was to modernize and allow more interactivity with the user. The goal was full adoption of the product to enhance their experience on the DART.

    I wanted to help the customers have a painless experience accessing their most frequent tasks and needs on the app.

    I started with low-fidelity wireframe sketches and then worked up to higher fidelity using Illustrator. I took a linear approach and sketched the ticketing page first because this was the primary use case and I wanted to materialize what I envisioned in my head since the research phase. Then I generated the layout and structure of the functional and data elements for the rest of the app.

    SITEMAP

    Once my mentor approved the content and functionality like a client would, I started to structure the content. Thanks to the sitemap, I wasn't totally lost in wireframing and used Jesse James Garrett's Visual Vocabulary to represent the architecture of the app. After identifying the main plot-points in the scenarios, I defined the primary pathways our personas would explore in the app. Developing several user stories for the personas was the best way to structure the content and functionality.

    INTERACTIVE DESIGN

    I spent the most time iterating the interactive portion of the app because I needed to make sure everything was connected correctly and the flows were intuitive. I inserted the wireframes in Invision to visualize how the app would interact with the users.

    The feedback I received was that I needed to show value at the beginning of the app instead of making the user register first. I needed to show why they should register first before making them start entering information.

    The feedback regarding the Transit Tracker for train and bus was that there was a lot of mental processing required of the user to remember what route they chose and which bus stop it was connected to. To improve the favorites use case, introducing a map UI was necessary to make the selection process easier and more visual. This was an issue most competitors had where users would end up in a blackhole of lists to find their station or stop.

    The ticketing page had issues of knowing what was clickable so I had to make the buttons visually obvious.

    VISUAL DESIGN

    I wanted to keep the general style and color of the original app of a blue and white color scheme. The header and button uses a gradient from dark blue to a lighter tone to distinguish from the rest of the elements on the page.

    I had to iterate a few times to get the color right making it darker. The font was chosen for its simplicity and readability.

    Prototyping

    Prototyping was the most effective way to gain meaningful feedback from my mentor. It showed what worked and what did not. The hardest part of prototyping the map UI was that I discovered one user's sense of "current location" is not the same as another user.

    USER TESTING

    To test the app, I asked 3 DART riders to try out the clickable prototype. I asked for their initial impressions and then had them perform specific tasks.

    Overall, participants found the app easy to use. All users were excited to see the inclusion of a visual map when referencing their bus or train stop.

    A few key areas of the app were confusing to users:
    They were confused which elements of the ticket page were clickable. Using Invision limited the availability of an actual interactive map for the users to navigate around, and could only provide screenshots.

    To correct these issues, I made the buttons on the ticket page look more like buttons and numbered the descriptions so users knew to start from the top down. There had to be some location disclaimer when performing the tasks of favoriting a stop or looking up a station. Due to time constraints, I was not able to make every station clickable and had to focus on a popular area to showcase how the app would've worked if it was built.

    FINAL DESIGNS

    After iterating on paper and reviewing with my manager, I was able to narrow down the concepts and start converting the sketches into wireframes in Sketch. After multiple rounds of iteration and review, I converted the designs into higher fidelity using our design system.

    • Utilized existing elements and brand colors
    • Emphasis on important information
    • Every notification has a follow-up action

    Purchasing a ticket is the most important feature so it needs to be efficient. First time users will need to register in this quick 3-step process only requiring a phone number.

    Transit Tracker is the perfect tool to search for a station or transit times. The favorites page allows users to quickly puchase saved ticket settings and access their most frequent stops for ETAs.

    Keeping some familiarity to the original app allows users to not get lost into the revised app. With newly added interactive maps, users can depend on prior experiences with Google Maps and alike apps to help them learn to use the new features.

    What I would've done differently

    • Define expectations and the role more clearly to avoid unexpected scope creep
    • Allow for more time to conduct deeper user research to better guide design decisions
